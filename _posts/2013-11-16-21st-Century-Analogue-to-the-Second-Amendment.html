Typically, I keep my distance from such polarised issues, since it only stands to encourage the incalculable amount of time spent deliberating comparatively trivial issues. However, in this case I think it’s worth putting the Second Amendment into its proper context.
If you think its primary focus is about preserving or restricting the right to a cellar full of guns, as I fear most people do, then sadly you’re missing the point entirely.
At its time of inception, the Second Amendment was about securing a balance of power between the government and its citizens. Historically - with many examples today - governments that have been able to mandate a great power imbalance in their favour, have have tended towards violence and oppression rather than safety and liberty.
In this sense, today’s debate over the Second Amendment should have nothing to do with guns; the debate should be about its principle: securing the balance of power. The 21st century balance of power doesn’t depend on weapons, it’s increasingly about information. To stay empowered, citizens need an explicit right to:
  • passive privacy (no surveillance or warrantless searches)
  • active privacy (encryption)
  • absolute freedom to transfer information (let's not shoot the messenger)
  • international protection from governments that don’t grant all of the above
In many ways, the West has been working towards restricting each of these freedoms for decades, and for the most part it’s going unnoticed, or being met with considerable apathy.
  • nobody is naive enough anymore to believe we’re not all being surveilled, yet the brief public backlash demanded nothing of the government
  • countries like Germany the UK have begun to outlaw encryption
  • British papers have started sending their articles to be published in America due to (democratically unprecedented) threats of criminal charges
  • every Western country is engaged in bi-lateral spying, information exchange, and extradition
So, while the US continues to battle over gun policy, take note of the war that’s increasingly being lost.
